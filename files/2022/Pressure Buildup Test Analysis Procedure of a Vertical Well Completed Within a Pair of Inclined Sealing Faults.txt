Abstract


Pressure buildup test analysis has been used to investigate near wellbore environment, estimate reservoir pressure and reveal external reservoir boundary types. However, despite of these benefits no consideration has been given for the angle of inclination of the faults or boundaries within which the well is completed. The angle of inclination of the boundaries of a reservoir has a significant impact on the pressure behavior and performance of a well. Knowledge of the inclination angle of the faults assist in well placement for optimum production.The objective of this paper, therefore, is to develop a procedure for calculating the angle of a well completed within a pair of inclined sealing faults using pressure buildup test analysis.A well completed in an infinite-acting reservoir produces number of images inversely proportional to the angle of inclination and dependent on nature of the faults. This number of images, n, can also be related to the angle at which the faults are inclined. The principle of superposition is used to aggregate pressure drop history of a vertical shutin after a constant rate of production. Pressure buildup test procedure was developed from the total pressure expression following the rate history.The procedure considered fault angle and distances from an object well of every image formed because of the inclination of the sealing faults.The buildup test procedure developed shows linear partial variation of Horner ratio at early shutin time and late shutin time, assuming constant rate of production before shut in. The coefficient of the early time Horner's ratio is the early flow time slope. The late time Horner's ratio has coefficient which is the product of the early time slope and the number of images formed due to sealing faults angle.The result shows that that the model is only valid for long shutin time, and the second slope must be gotten at the late time region for higher accuracy. Finally, the procedure does not depend on well design or distances of the images from the object well.The analysis procedure developed here can be used to estimate the angle of inclination of two faults within which a well is completed. Near wellbore characterization is also possible. Decision on well location for optimum well production or injection can be made from the angle of inclination of the faults.

Introduction


A reservoir boundary can be a fault which surrounds a reservoir and causes discontinuity of the reservoir or indicate where the reservoir ends. Some of the types of reservoir boundaries includes constant pressure boundary, closed boundary and infinite actiung reservoir.


An infinite acting reservoir is a reservoir without any physical boundary. Therefore, boundary effects will not appear on the analysis plots during the duration of the test.


A no-flow or closed boundary is a boundary that does not allow flow through it. This kind of boundary usually occurs in reservoirs with sealing faults or is created between producing wells that are equally spaced and producing at the same rate. Pseudo-steady state flow signifies that all no-flow boundaries have been reached (Gerard, M 1985).


The presence of faults in the reservoir have continued to play an important role in the exploitation of petroleum resources from subsurface reservoirs as the identification of these fault structures through well test analyses provide significant boost for optimum production output of petroleum (Horner, 1951, Perrine, R. L., 1956, Overpeck, A.C et al, 1970).


When a reservoir is bounded, production from the well is affected in the long term according to the nature of the boundary. The length of time for oil production is strongly affected by well placement with respect to the boundary, whether the boundaries are single, paired and vertical or paired and inclined. It is therefore important that well placement is guided to achieve long term oil production (Gerard, M. et al., 1985). Good well location may be decided from solution to a flow equation describing pressure distribution (Davis et al. 2004). The solution shows flow rates and well location for available reservoir system properties.


Well test analysis has been widely used as an industry standard to determine reservoir boundaries (Davis, E.G. et al, 1963, Prasad, R.K. 1975, Britton, P.R. et al, A.S., 1988). The boundaries present in reservoirs take the form of sealing or non-sealing faults (Tiab, D, et al, 1980). The identification of these faults will enable engineers to determine well placement for optimal production of petroleum reservoirs, as well as provide knowledge on their long-term performance


In this study, pressure buildup test analysis procedure of a vertical oil well completed with a pair of inclined sealing faults is studied to estimate the angle of inclination of the sealing faults. Simulated well test data will be utilized to demonstrate application of the test analysis procedure developed in this paper.

Given that a well is completed in a reservoir bounded by inclined at angle θ as shown in Figure 1.

A well completed in an finite-acting reservoir produces a number of images inversely proportional to the angle of inclination as shown in Equation 1.


n=360θ−1(1)


This number of images, n, can also be related to the angle at which the well is inclined. The principle of superposition is used to aggregate pressure drop history of a vertical shutin after a constant rate of production. Pressure buildup test procedure is developed from the total pressure expression following the rate history (Ramey, H.J., Jr. et al, 1968). The procedure considers fault angle and distances of all image wells from an object well.


Rate and Pressure Profiles of Pressure Buildup Testing


The well produces at a constant rate, q1, for time tp hours, during which at this time the flowing wellbore pressure stabilizes. The well is shut in for time Δt hours. Observed stabilized wellbore pressure is recorded. Also recorded are observed shut in pressures. The rate and pressure profiles for this procedure is shown in Figure 2. The history implies that q1 has been in effect for time (tp+Δt) hours, while the q2 has been in influence for time Δt hours (Earlougher, R.C. Jr. 1977), Matthews, C.S and Russell, D.G., (1967).

For a well completed within a pair of sealing (no flow) boundaries, the object and image wells experience pressure drops proportional to object well rates. Therefore, from principle of superposition (Tiab, D. and Kumar, A., 1980, Prasad, R.K., 1975, Adewole, E.S. 2021), the total pressure drop in the object well as a result of flow in the object well, ΔpT,o, and all image wells, is


ΔpTT,o=Δp in the object well due to flow in the object well +Δp in the object well due to flow in 1st image well at a distance d1 from the object well +Δp in the object well due to flow in the 2nd  image well at a distance d2 from the object well+………….+Δp in the object well due to flow in the nth image wellat a distance dn from the object well(2)

Or


μΔpT,o=162.6q1μBkh[logtp+ΔtΔt+logtp+ΔtΔtn](8)

Finally, since the well has been shut in, Equation 9 is written as


pws=pi−m [logtp+ΔtΔt+nlogtp+ΔtΔt](10)


where


μm=162.6q1μBkh(11)


Equation 10 is the pressure buildup relationship required for analysis of pressure buildup test for a well completed within a pair of no-flow boundaries. If pws is plotted on a semilog paper against the Horner's ratio, (tp+Δt)/ Δt, it will produce a slope (m+nm).


It can be seen that the pressure buildup procedure does not depend on well design or the distances of image wells from the object well but depends on two Horner's ratios and the number of images formed by the inclination of sealing faults, the initial reservoir pressure and fluid and reservoir properties.


Results and Discussion


A reservoir system was simulated using Equation 10 by first assuming different angles of inclination. Fluid and reservoir properties were also simulated. The value of n was then computed using Equation 1 for each set of silumated data. Then shutin pressures (pws) were computed. A plot of the shutin pressure (pws) against Horner's ratio was made for each set of simulated data.


The value of n gotten was then compared with the initial assumed value to confirm the validity of the procedure. Results obtained are shown in Tables 1 to 11 and Figures 3 to 11.

The result of the analysis presented above has shown the angle of inclination of a fault in the reservoir using the different well data which is the main aim of this study, and it is important for well placement to improve reservoir performance and production.


From the tables and plots above, the model is valid for long shutin times as all values of n is same or matches with the previously assumed values.


The slopes were all gotten at the very late time region because that is when the fault begins to exert its influence. The buildup test procedure developed shows linear partial variation of reservoir pressure and Horner's ratio at early shutin time and late shutin time, assuming constant rate of production before shut in. The coefficient of the early time Horner's ratio is the early slope. The late time Horner's ratio has coefficient which is the product of the early time slope and the number of images formed due to sealing fault angle. Furthermore, the procedure does not depend on well design and image distances of the images from the object well. Reservoir boundaries influence pressure behavior of the well and its performance during buildup (Martinez, S. et al, 1985). Once the angle of inclination of the reservoir is known it is therefore necessary to make sure the well is placed far away from the boundary.


Conclusion


In this study, a mathematical model for calculating the angle of inclination of a well completed within a pair of inclined sealing faults using pressure buildup procedure has been developed successfully.The model was also tested with well and reservoir data to check for authenticityIt can be seen from the results that the model is only valid for long shut in time and the second slope must be gotten at the late time region for better accuracy.Decision on well location for optimum well production or injection can be made from the angle of inclination of the fault in the reservoir once it is known.The buildup procedure developed does not depend on well design. Image well distances does not also affect pressure buildup in the well bore.
